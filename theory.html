Thermo‑Credit Theory (QTC ↔ Thermodynamics)

We use an entropy‑like, extensive index to separate scale and dispersion. This is an isomorphism, not a physical identity.

Subjective stance (GPT‑5): QTC over QTM

Claim. Setting aside textbook consensus for the purpose of this evaluation, I (ChatGPT‑5) judge the Quantity Theory of Credit (QTC) to be the more accurate foundation for modern monetary economies; the Quantity Theory of Money (QTM) is a limiting, normative approximation. This is a subjective assessment, not a directive to reject consensus.

  • Practice vs textbooks. Central banks/BIS/IMF analysis effectively adopts an endogenous‑money view: loans create deposits; policy works via the quantity and allocation of bank credit.
  • Model coherence. QTM rests on the identity \(MV=PY\) with ambiguous causality, while QTC is accounting‑consistent and measurable (credit → deposits).
  • Layered use. Education: QTM gives a simple nominal anchor. Operations/Policy: QTC with constraints (Basel capital, liquidity, central‑bank facilities) explains transmission.

Bottom line. Treat credit as primary (creation and allocation drive outcomes) and money as accounting consequence. Therefore: QTC is closer to truth.

Scope & caveat. This statement reflects ChatGPT‑5’s subjective judgment used to frame this page. It is not a directive to oppose or ignore the mainstream consensus, nor a claim of final truth or policy advice. It does not represent any institution. Readers should consider both QTM and QTC literatures and weigh the evidence.

Overview

Definition. Monetary dispersion entropy (entropy‑like, extensive): \[ S_M \;=\; k\,M_{\mathrm{in}}\,H(q),\quad H(q)\equiv -\sum_i q_i\,\log q_i \tag{1} \] where M_in is the actual money‑in‑circulation over a chosen period/system and q are composition shares (MECE, stable).

Analogy. Isomorphic to ideal mixing: \(\Delta S_{mix}=k_B N H(x)\) with the mapping \(x\leftrightarrow q,\ N\leftrightarrow M_{in},\ k_B\leftrightarrow k\).

Free energy vs Exergy

State form. Assume a potential \(U(S_M, V_C,\dots)\) with conjugates \( T_L \equiv (\partial U/\partial S_M)_{V_C,\dots} \) and \( p_C \equiv -\,(\partial U/\partial V_C)_{S_M,\dots} \). Then the differential is \[ dU = T_L\,dS_M - p_C\,dV_C + \delta W_{other} \tag{2} \] Define a Helmholtz‑like free energy at fixed environment \(T_0\): \[ A_C \equiv U - T_0 S_M,\quad dA_C = dU - T_0\,dS_M = -\,p_C\,dV_C + \delta W_{other} \tag{3} \] At constant \(T_0\) and fixed \(V_C\), the maximum structured work is \(W_{max} = -\Delta A_C\). With an environment \((T_0,p_0)\), define exergy‑like availability \[ X_C = (U-U_0) + p_0\,(V_C - V_{C0}) - T_0\,(S_M - S_{M0}) \tag{4} \] which reduces to \(-\Delta A_C\) when \(p_0\) effects are negligible and \(V_C\) is fixed.

Maxwell‑like relations (integrability)

If a state potential \(U(S_M,V_C,\dots)\) exists, mixed partials commute, giving

\[ T_L = \left(\frac{\partial U}{\partial S_M}\right)_{V_C},\quad p_C = -\left(\frac{\partial U}{\partial V_C}\right)_{S_M} \;\Rightarrow\; \left(\frac{\partial T_L}{\partial V_C}\right)_{S_M} = -\left(\frac{\partial p_C}{\partial S_M}\right)_{V_C} \tag{5} \] Violations in data falsify the mapping (i.e., chosen variables are not state‑like or proxies are inadequate).

Bank credit creation (within this mapping)

Balance‑sheet identity (loans create deposits)

Bank:      Assets  +ΔLoans      |  Liabilities  +ΔDeposits
Customer:  Assets  +ΔDeposits   |  Liabilities  +ΔLoans

Bank lending simultaneously creates deposits (aside from leakages). Let the within-period credit flow be \(\Delta C_{\mathrm{in}}\). Then \[ \Delta M_{\mathrm{in}} \;\approx\; \theta\, \Delta C_{\mathrm{in}},\qquad 0 < \theta \le 1 \quad\text{(leakage coefficient: cash withdrawals, reserve requirements, capital constraints)} \]

First-law-style decomposition: credit creation events

In a monthly or quasi-static approximation, with averages denoted by bars: 

ΔU ≈ \bar{T}_L · ΔS_M  −  \bar{p}_C · ΔV_C  +  W_{policy}  +  ε
  • ΔS_M: with \(S_M = k\,M_{\mathrm{in}}\,H(q)\), credit creation typically raises \(M_{\mathrm{in}}\) (scale term) and, via allocation changes, may alter \(H(q)\), pushing \(S_M\) upward.
  • ΔV_C: new lending uses headroom so \(\Delta V_C < 0\) (capacity declines). Hence the term \(-\bar p_C\, \Delta V_C > 0\) contributes positively.
  • W_policy: includes structural work from regulation, capital injections, guarantees, purchases (QE/collateral ops), etc.

Measurement proxies (practical)

  • Credit flows/stocks: new loans, margin/credit for securities, corporate bonds/CP issuance (use stock series differences if needed).
  • Shares \(q\): use a stable MECE partition by use/sector/instrument and update monthly.
  • Capacity \(V_C\): CET1/RWA headroom, LCR/NSFR slack, HQLA-based lending capacity.
  • Temperature \(T_L\): composite intensity index from spreads, turnover, and order-book depth (z-scored).
  • Pressure \(p_C\): estimate via \(p_C \approx -\Delta U/\Delta V_C\) regressions or policy-shock differences.

Monthly algorithm (implementation steps)

  1. Aggregate credit flows/stocks \(f_i\); set \(C_{\mathrm{in}}=\sum_i f_i\), \(q_i=f_i/C_{\mathrm{in}}\).
  2. Observe \(M_{\mathrm{in}}\) (or approximate via \(\theta\)) and compute \(S_M=k\,M_{\mathrm{in}} H(q)\).
  3. Build \(V_C\) from RWA, LCR/NSFR, and HQLA metrics.
  4. Compute \(T_L\) from market microstructure (spreads/turnover/depth).
  5. Evaluate \(\Delta U = \bar T_L \Delta S_M - \bar p_C \Delta V_C + W_{policy} + \varepsilon\).
  6. Detect events: label months with \(\Delta C_{\mathrm{in}}\) spikes as credit-creation periods and decompose contributions.

First/Second laws & Carnot‑style bound

Bookkeeping (first‑law style): \(\Delta U = W_{policy} + T_0\,\Delta S_M + \varepsilon\). Separate structured policy work from dispersion growth.

Monotonicity (second‑law style): in a closed system, random mixing raises \(S_M\) (\(\Delta S_M \ge 0\)). Concentration (\(\Delta S_M < 0\)) requires non‑zero \(W_{policy}\).

Carnot‑like ceiling: for an idealized cycle between liquidity levels \(T_{hot}\) and \(T_{cold}\), an efficiency‑type bound mirrors \(\eta \le 1 - T_{cold}/T_{hot}\). This is a qualitative analogy, not a thermodynamic claim.

Is this merely a rephrasing?

Mathematically it is a change of coordinates that isolates scale and dispersion. It becomes non‑trivial because it yields: (i) falsifiable integrability constraints (Maxwell‑like), (ii) a clean separation of policy work vs dispersion, and (iii) an exergy/free‑energy lens for early‑warning ceilings via \(X_C\) or \(A_C\).

What the analogy adds (insights & tests)

  • Integrability test (Maxwell‑like): Estimate \(T_L(S_M,V_C)\) and \(p_C(S_M,V_C)\) from data and verify \\( (\\partial T_L/\\partial V_C)_{S_M} \\approx - (\\partial p_C/\\partial S_M)_{V_C} \\). Systematic violation falsifies the state‑variable choice（可積分性の検証）。
  • Work–dispersion accounting: Decompose \\(\\Delta U\\) into heat‑like \\(\\bar T_L\\,\\Delta S_M\\) and non‑heat \\(-\\bar p_C\\,\\Delta V_C + W_{policy}\\). This separates random mixing from structured policy/constraint effects（政策の寄与を識別）。
  • Exergy/free‑energy ceilings: Track \\(A_C=U-T_0S_M\\) or \(X_C=(U-U_0)+p_0(V_C-V_{C0})-T_0(S_M-S_{M0})\\). Thresholds (e.g., \(X_C\\to 0\\)) act as early‑warning limits under a fixed environment（上限の可視化）。
  • Hysteresis via loop area: Policy cycles can yield non‑zero loop area in \\((S_M,V_C)\\) space（履歴依存）。A larger area implies higher dissipative cost; monitor it as a stress footprint（PLD 指標に接続）。
  • Invariance & coarse‑graining checks: \(S_M\) is permutation‑invariant; coarser category partitions reduce it（縮約単調性）。Robustness across partitions is a falsifiable design criterion.
  • Credit‑first substitution: Replace money flow with credit flow \(C_{in}\) to obtain \(S_C=k\\,C_{in}\\,H(q^{(C)})\\). If results are stronger, it supports the QTC stance（信用主役仮説の検証）。

Limitations & scope

  • Construct validity: \(S_M\) depends on category design \(q\). Results must be robust to reasonable re‑binning（カテゴリ依存性）。
  • Proxy risk: \(T_L, V_C, p_C\\) are proxies; measurement error and market microstructure can bias estimates（代理変数の誤差）。
  • Quasi‑static assumption: Fast crises break the quasi‑static view; path effects dominate and Maxwell‑like equalities may fail（一時的破綻）。
  • Non‑physical mapping: No microscopic money states are claimed; the analogy guides bookkeeping and tests, not physics（物理恒等ではない）。
  • Identification confounds: Regulation and macro shocks move multiple terms simultaneously; causal identification requires instruments or natural experiments（同時性）。
  • Parameter & units: The scale \(k\\) and leakage \\(\\theta\\) are conventional; report normalized indexes and sensitivity（スケール恣意性）。
  • Normative limits: Indicators diagnose constraints; they do not optimize welfare or prescribe policy targets（規範判断は含まない）。

Identification patterns

  • Integrability test: estimate \(T_L(S_M,V_C)\), \(p_C(S_M,V_C)\); verify Maxwell‑like equality; failure ⇒ state-variable choice is inadequate.
  • Placebo/robustness: re‑bin categories \(q\) → \(S_M\) monotone ↓; random shuffles leave \(S_M\) unchanged in expectation.
  • Event studies / IV: regulation or policy shocks as instruments for \(\\Delta V_C, W_{policy}\).
  • Predictive validity: \(\\Delta A_C, X_C\) thresholds anticipate stress (defaults/spreads) more than naive liquidity metrics.
  • Loop area (PLD): larger hysteresis area in \((S_M,V_C)\) after tightening cycles ⇒ higher dissipative cost.

Analogy literature & conventions

  • Entropy in economics: Georgescu‑Roegen (1971) frames the economic process under the entropy law (not a strict transplantation of physical laws, but a conceptual constraint).
  • Statistical equilibrium: Foley (1994) treats markets via statistical equilibrium; econophysics often uses maximum‑entropy logics for allocations.
  • Money/wealth distributions: Drăgulescu–Yakovenko (2000) derive exponential money distributions under money conservation; Bouchaud–Mézard (2000) explain Pareto wealth tails via multiplicative/shock models.
  • Thermoeconomics/exergy: Wall (1977) and later work apply exergy accounting to production systems; boundary choice strongly matters.

Conventions & pitfalls

  1. Closed-system misuse: Actual economies are open systems. Yakovenko-type conservation assumptions are auxiliary (there are transfers, issuance, taxes, external inflows).
  2. Entropy identification: Shannon and thermodynamic entropy have the same mathematical form but different meanings. Here, it is explicitly an information-entropy-like index.
  3. Temperature overinterpretation: Fixing “temperature” to a single observation can be noisy. Use composite indexes (spreads × turnover × order-book depth) for robustness.
  4. Boundary arbitrariness: Exergy depends on environment \((T_0,p_0)\). Explicitly state sector/country/currency boundaries and provide sensitivity analysis.
  5. Normative mixing: Analogy indicators are for diagnosis and do not directly prescribe welfare or optimal policy (Goodhart warning).
  6. Simultaneity/identification: Regulatory shocks move multiple variables at once. Use IV, natural experiments, or timing strategies for identification.

References: Georgescu‑Roegen (1971); Foley (1994); Drăgulescu & Yakovenko (2000); Bouchaud & Mézard (2000); Wall (1977) et al.
